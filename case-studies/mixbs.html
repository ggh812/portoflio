01 — Brief

MiXBS was running one of Europe's largest BPO operations on a patchwork of third-party tools that didn't share data, couldn't forecast together, and gave operations managers no unified view of the business.

By 2022, MiXBS handled upwards of 14,000 customer interactions daily across healthcare, logistics, retail, and e-commerce clients. Automation, workforce management, and performance analytics were each handled by separate third-party systems — none of which integrated cleanly, and all of which required specialist knowledge to configure or interpret. Workforce planners built schedules in Excel because the WFM tool was too rigid to model their actual shift patterns. Automation rules were authored by IT rather than operations staff, creating a bottleneck every time a client's contact flow needed adjustment. Analytics were consumed as weekly PDF exports, not live operational signals.

The decision to build BayCX — a proprietary platform unifying all three capabilities — came from a strategic need to own the intelligence layer of their operation. As an external UX consultant, I was brought in to define the product from the ground up: leading discovery across three distinct user groups, establishing the platform architecture and design system, and taking each module from concept through to launched product over three years.

02 — Discovery

Eight weeks across three distinct user groups — each with their own domain expertise, mental models, and failure modes when tooling didn't serve them.

The three platform modules served fundamentally different audiences: operations supervisors configuring and monitoring automation; workforce planners modelling demand and building schedules; and analytics users — a mix of ops managers and client account managers — who needed to turn raw interaction data into decisions. Understanding each group required different research methods and a willingness to go deep into genuinely specialised domains.

Supervisor Walkthroughs
24 shadowing sessions with operations supervisors across live contact floors — observing how they monitored queue states, intervened on automation failures, and escalated edge cases in real time.
WFM Specialist Sessions
12 structured interviews and task-observation sessions with workforce planners. We mapped their existing Excel-based processes step by step — every formula, every workaround — before designing the WFM module to replace them.
Automation Rule Authoring
18 co-creation sessions where ops staff attempted to configure automation rules using prototype interfaces. Exposed the gap between how non-technical users think about interaction flows and how rule engines typically model them.
Analytics Comprehension Testing
Moderated testing sessions with 14 ops managers and client account contacts — presenting existing data exports and asking them to answer specific business questions. Mapped where interpretation failed and why.

Key Research Findings

1
Workforce planners had abandoned the WFM tool in favour of Excel
The existing third-party WFM system couldn't model the multi-skill, multi-shift patterns MiXBS operated. Planners had created sophisticated Excel models over years — with colour-coded shift types, manual forecast adjustments, and holiday override logic — because no tool gave them the flexibility they needed. The design challenge wasn't to replicate Excel; it was to understand what made their Excel models work and build that reasoning directly into BayCX's WFM module.
2
Automation configuration was gated behind IT — creating a two-week bottleneck for every change
Contact flow adjustments — new IVR options, updated chatbot responses, modified routing rules — required a ticket to IT, an average two-week turnaround, and no visibility on progress. Operations supervisors had deep knowledge of what needed to change and why, but no interface that let them act on it. Every delay had a measurable cost: misrouted interactions, outdated responses, and client complaints about stale contact experiences.
3
Analytics were consumed weekly — by which point the window to act had passed
Weekly PDF performance reports arrived in inboxes every Monday. By the time managers reviewed them, the preceding week's staffing problems, automation failures, and quality drops were already history. The data was accurate but operationally useless at that latency. What managers actually needed was a live signal layer — not more historical reporting — that surfaced emerging issues within hours, not days.
4
No single view existed across automation, staffing, and performance
When something went wrong — a volume spike, a quality dip, a rash of automation failures — ops managers had to open three separate tools to diagnose the root cause. The absence of cross-module correlation meant that obvious relationships (high automation failure rates correlating with understaffing downstream) were invisible at the surface level, requiring manual cross-referencing of data sets that should have been connected by default.
03 — Platform

Three modules sharing one data layer — designed so that what happens in automation is visible in workforce planning, and what workforce planning forecasts feeds the analytics layer.

The core architectural decision was that BayCX's three modules couldn't be designed as separate products. Each module's output feeds the next: automation performance data informs WFM demand forecasting; WFM schedules determine capacity available for automation escalations; and the analytics layer surfaces patterns across both. I worked with MiXBS's engineering team across the first year of the engagement to define the data model that would make cross-module intelligence possible before any UI work began.

Module 01
Interaction Automation
Visual rule builder for configuring IVR flows, chatbot responses, and automated routing — operable by ops staff without IT involvement. Real-time automation health monitoring with human-handoff rate tracking.
Module 02

Module 03

Live operations dashboard surfacing emerging issues — volume spikes, quality drops, automation failure clusters — before they become reportable problems. Correlation view across all three modules in a single screen.

04 — WFM Module

Workforce management is one of the most domain-specific design challenges in enterprise SaaS — the gap between what planners know and what most tools offer is where scheduling quality is lost.

The WFM module had to do something genuinely difficult: replace a highly personalised, years-evolved Excel process without removing the flexibility that made that process work. The key insight from planner sessions was that scheduling isn't a single-step action — it's an iterative conversation between a demand forecast, a set of constraints, and a pool of agents with different skills, availability, and preferences. Most WFM tools collapse this into a one-shot generate button. BayCX needed to expose the reasoning.

I designed the WFM module around three distinct planning phases — forecast, build, monitor — each with its own interface state. The forecast phase surfaces historical patterns and anomaly flags before a schedule is generated. The build phase allows planners to adjust the generated schedule against constraint overrides, seeing the impact on coverage in real time. The monitor phase tracks live adherence against that plan and surfaces deviations before they affect SLA.

05 — Analytics

Turning raw interaction data into operational decisions — not by adding more charts, but by surfacing the right signal at the right moment.

The analytics comprehension testing exposed a consistent problem: every manager we tested could read a chart, but almost none could translate what they were seeing into an action they should take right now. The data was accurate but inactionable at the level it was presented. Metrics existed in isolation — a quality score here, a handle time there — with no connection to why the number had moved or what could be done about it.

The analytics module was designed around a different principle: show the anomaly first, then the data that explains it. The primary view is a live operations feed that surfaces cross-module deviations — interactions where automation failure correlates with downstream agent overload, or where a quality dip is traced to a specific shift segment that's understaffed. Numbers are shown only in the context of their target range and recent trend, never as standalone figures requiring interpretation.
