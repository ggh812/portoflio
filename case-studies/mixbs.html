01 — Challenge

BayCX was built to manage operations — not to support the people doing the work.

MiXBS handles upwards of 14,000 customer interactions daily across healthcare, logistics, retail, and e-commerce clients. Their proprietary platform, BayCX, orchestrates every agent shift: live call management, customer data lookup, script delivery, quality monitoring, and client reporting — all in one tool. But BayCX had been built iteratively by engineering, shaped around operational metrics and integration requirements rather than the cognitive reality of front-line contact work.

By 2022, the consequences were measurable. Agents were juggling four open interface panels during a live call. New hires required three or more weeks of supervised practice before handling calls independently. Client account managers were logging into a reporting dashboard they found largely uninterpretable, generating manual exports they then reformatted in spreadsheets. As MiXBS scaled toward 4,000 team members, the platform's design debt had become a direct operational cost — in agent turnover, onboarding overhead, and client retention risk.

I came in as an external UX consultant in 2022 to audit the existing BayCX experience, identify the highest-impact opportunities across both agent-facing and client-facing interfaces, and lead the design transformation over a sustained engagement spanning three years.

02 — Research

Six weeks of discovery — agent desk shadowing, client interviews, and a full heuristic audit before a single design decision was made.

Contact centre UX research requires access that most environments don't easily grant: live call floors during peak hours, account manager quarterly reviews, escalation queues. MiXBS opened access across their teams, and the picture that emerged was consistent and urgent.

Agent Desk Shadowing
28 live call observation sessions across healthcare, logistics, and retail queues. Captured real screen navigation patterns, workarounds, and multi-window management during active calls.
Client Portal Interviews
14 interviews with client account managers across MiXBS's major verticals. All described relying on manual exports and spreadsheet reformatting to make reporting data usable in their own reviews.
Support Ticket Analysis
Review of 600 internal support tickets from the previous 12 months. 61% related to BayCX navigation confusion, panel state management, or data lost during call transitions.
Heuristic Evaluation
Full Nielsen heuristic review of the BayCX agent workspace and client portal. 87 violations catalogued — 23 critical. Primary failure modes: visibility of system status, error recovery, and real-world alignment.

Key Research Findings

1
Agents routinely navigated 4+ open interface panels during a live call
Every call type required switching between the active call panel, customer data lookup, script and knowledge base, and ticket log — in separate browser windows with no shared state. A single misclick could collapse the view, requiring agents to rebuild context while the customer waited. Observation sessions showed agents spending 35–45% of call time on internal navigation rather than customer resolution.
2
Client reporting was designed for data export, not for decision-making
The client portal surfaced 40+ metrics across eight dashboard tabs — no hierarchy, no trends, no explanation of what each metric meant in practice. Every client interviewee described an identical workaround: export raw data, reformat in a spreadsheet, then present internally. One account manager estimated spending six hours per month reformatting data that should have been readable in the portal directly. The workaround had become so universal it was invisible.
3
Onboarding was a 3-week supervised handhold — and the tool was the reason
New agents didn't struggle with call handling or script knowledge — they struggled with the tool. Trainers described the same pattern consistently: the first week was almost entirely BayCX navigation, with actual call handling starting in week two. Absent progressive disclosure, onboarding states, or visual consistency across panels, there was no gradual path from trainee to independent. Agents either memorised the interface or remained dependent on senior colleagues.
4
Error states were invisible until it was too late to correct them
BayCX's call logging system allowed agents to submit incomplete records silently — no validation, no confirmation state, no distinction between "saved" and "submitted." Quality monitoring sessions found 18% of call records were missing required fields at submission, discovered only during the next day's audit when correction was significantly more costly. The absence of real-time status feedback was the single highest-impact heuristic failure.
03 — Agent Workspace

Collapsing four fragmented panels into one unified workspace — without disrupting the operational data architecture underneath.

BayCX's panel architecture reflected real integration boundaries — the telephony system, CRM, knowledge base, and ticket logger were each separate services. A unified front-end couldn't change the back-end topology; it had to surface all four data sources coherently within a single interface session. I worked with MiXBS's engineering team over four months to map every real-time data dependency between panels, identify where state could be shared, and design a contextual workspace where the dominant panel shifted automatically based on call phase.

Each phase — incoming call, active conversation, post-call wrap — surfaced only what was needed at that moment. The customer lookup triggered on call connect. The script panel anchored the active call view. The wrap-up screen gated submission behind real-time field validation, making incomplete records impossible to submit silently.

04 — Client Portal

Forty metrics across eight tabs — redesigned around the three questions every client actually needs to answer each week.

Client interviews made the diagnostic clear: the existing portal was a data lake wearing dashboard clothes. Every metric BayCX captured was surfaced, with no hierarchy, no temporal context, and no interpretation. Clients who needed to answer "is my team performing to SLA this week?" were presented with 40+ raw figures and no path to an answer without exporting and reformatting the data themselves.

The redesign started from the questions, not the data. Through structured interviews with 14 client contacts, I identified three core weekly questions every client tried to answer: performance against SLA targets, quality score trends, and volume patterns. The new portal gave each question its own view, with supporting data available on demand rather than by default — and with inline context explaining what each metric meant and why it mattered.
