01 — Context

In Austria, every cattle movement — birth, death, farm transfer, import, export — must be reported to the national Veterinärinformationssystem (VIS) within 7 days. Non-compliance risks EU subsidy penalties and livestock disease tracing failures.

Rindernet is Austria's official cattle registration network, managing the registration of approximately 2.0 million cattle across 18,000+ farms. Until 2023, the primary reporting mechanism was paper-based forms or a desktop-only web portal that was last redesigned in 2014.

The problems were systemic: farmers filled in paper forms in their barns, sometimes days after the reportable event, then posted or faxed them to regional offices. Data entry staff transcribed these manually into VIS, introducing a second layer of transcription errors. Average reporting lag was 4.2 days — well within the 7-day window but with peak-season lag (spring calving) frequently exceeding 6 days for high-volume farms.

EU Regulation (EU) 2019/2035 required Austria to demonstrate improved traceability by Q1 2025 — creating a hard deadline for the digital transformation programme that Rinderzucht Austria and the Federal Ministry of Agriculture (BMLRT) commissioned us to lead.

The design problem in one sentence: Create an app that a 65-year-old cattle farmer, standing in a dark barn with cold hands and no phone signal, can use to correctly report a calf birth in under 2 minutes — first time, without calling the helpline.

02 — Systems Mapping

Before designing a single screen, we had to understand — and document — a 14-party data ecosystem.

The Rindernet reporting system touches 14 distinct institutional actors: the farmer, regional cattle breeders' associations, AMA, the BMLRT, veterinary authorities across 9 provinces, border inspection posts, abattoirs, and the EU Commission's TRACES animal movement system.

A service blueprint spanning 3.2 metres of wall revealed the extraordinary complexity of back-office processes that the app needed to accommodate — while presenting the farmer with a deceptively simple interface.

03 — Accessibility

Designing for gloves, sunlight, older users, and intermittent connectivity isn't accessibility as compliance — it's accessibility as product strategy.

Standard WCAG 2.1 compliance was a baseline requirement, but the real accessibility challenge was the environmental context of use. We developed what we called "barn mode" design principles — a set of constraints applied to every UI decision:

Glove-First Touch Targets
All interactive elements minimum 56×56px. Primary actions 64px+. No swipe-only interactions on critical paths.
High-Contrast Sunlight Mode
Contrast ratios 7:1+ for all text. Auto-switching high-contrast mode triggered by ambient light sensor threshold.
Large Text & Simple Language
Minimum 16px body text. Plain German (Leichte Sprache principles for key instructions). No jargon without plain-language explanations.
Full Offline Capability
All core reporting tasks work with zero connectivity. Clear offline state indicators. Zero data loss guarantee — local storage with encrypted SQLite queue.
