01 — Challenge

The portal through which Austria administers billions in EU agricultural payments had never been designed — it had accumulated.

eAMA.at is the primary interface between Austria's 165,000+ farming operations and the state — the platform through which farmers submit the annual Mehrfachantrag (MFA), declare crops and field parcels, manage ÖPUL agri-environmental programme participation, and access livestock records. Over fifteen years of incremental development, the portal had evolved into a patchwork of disconnected modules: the MFA workflow, ÖPUL applications, cattle registration data, and market reports each operated with their own navigation structure, visual language, and technical constraints.

The consequences were acute and measurable. During the annual MFA submission window — a six-week period in April and May when all 165,000 operations are racing the same deadline — AMA's helpdesk volume spiked dramatically, with tens of thousands of calls relating not to eligibility questions, but to basic navigation uncertainty. Agricultural consultants estimated they were completing 30–40% of all MFA applications on behalf of farmers who could not navigate the system independently — an invisible cost borne by both parties.

On mobile, the situation was worse. The most time-critical workflow — the GIS parcel viewer, where farmers draw and confirm field boundaries — was built exclusively for desktop, with no touch-optimised interaction. Farmers accessing eAMA from tablets in the field, the most natural context for boundary declarations, were effectively locked out of the most important step.

02 — Research

Ten weeks of discovery across farm sites, AMA offices, and agricultural consultancy practices — spanning three Austrian regions and two growing seasons.

The farming user base presented a distinctive research challenge: a wide age range (significant proportion of operators over 55), high seasonal pressure concentrated into a six-week MFA window, genuinely varied digital literacy, and real-world contexts — barns, fields, muddy equipment — that made standard usability lab methods largely irrelevant.

Farm-Site Contextual Inquiry
32 on-farm observation sessions across Niederösterreich, Steiermark, and Burgenland. Captured real interaction patterns including glove use, sunlight, and tablet positioning in the field.
Consultant Interviews
18 interviews with agricultural consultants (Landwirtschaftskammer advisors) who regularly complete MFA applications on behalf of clients. Surface-level proxy for farmer intent data.
Helpdesk Call Analysis
Qualitative analysis of 800 transcribed helpdesk calls from MFA season 2020. Categorised by root cause — 73% were navigation or orientation failures, not process or eligibility questions.
Accessibility Audit
Full WCAG 2.1 review against AA criteria. Screen reader testing with NVDA and VoiceOver. 112 violations catalogued — priority-weighted against the MFA submission flow.
Analytics Review
Two-year session data review. Identified the exact steps in the MFA flow where drop-off clustered, peak-hour traffic patterns during deadline week, and the 14 most common error-state entries.

Key Research Findings

1
The MFA flow spanned 4 separate modules with no shared state
Farmers had to navigate between the parcel declaration module, the crop declaration module, the ÖPUL measure selector, and the final submission screen — each with its own URL, session, and navigation chrome. If they lost their place, there was no "where was I" mechanism. 44% of sessions during MFA season involved at least one module re-entry after abandonment.
2
Agricultural consultants had become a workaround for the portal's usability debt
Interviews with Landwirtschaftskammer advisors revealed that completing eAMA applications on behalf of clients had become a routine service — not for complex edge cases, but for standard MFA submissions. One consultant described completing applications for 120 clients annually, spending an average of 35 minutes per farm. This represented a structural cost invisible to AMA's usability metrics.
3
The GIS parcel viewer — the most critical component — was desktop-only
54% of sessions were on mobile or tablet, and usage was growing year-on-year. The parcel boundary tool, which farmers use to confirm and adjust their field geometries, was built on a desktop mapping library with no touch event handling. On tablets in the field — the most natural context for parcel confirmation — the interaction was completely non-functional.
4
112 WCAG violations — concentrated in the highest-traffic flows
The most critical: form labels missing on 14 MFA input fields, colour contrast failures across table cells (contrast ratio 2.8:1 against the green header), keyboard traps in the parcel selector, and a base font size of 12px throughout the data tables. Given the user base age profile — approximately 40% of active farms operated by those over 55 — these were not edge cases.
02b — IA Audit

Before redesigning a single screen, we mapped every reachable state in the existing portal — the full scope of what farmers were expected to navigate.

The IA audit produced a complete sitemap of the legacy eAMA system: every module, sub-module, and terminal state. The diagram exposed the structural fragmentation that research had surfaced anecdotally — eight top-level modules, no shared navigation model, and 140+ distinct screens that a farmer submitting the MFA might theoretically encounter.

Highlighted in red are the navigation dead-ends where users had no clear path forward. Blue nodes mark the duplicate entry points — the same data input appearing in multiple modules with no reconciliation between them. The audit formed the evidence base for the structural consolidation that followed.

03 — Process Design

Unifying four disconnected modules into a single, resumable submission flow — without disrupting the regulatory data model underneath.

The core structural challenge was that the MFA's four-module architecture reflected real administrative boundaries — parcel data, crop declarations, agri-environment measures, and submission acknowledgement are managed by separate systems internally. The redesign needed to create a unified front-end experience while preserving those back-end boundaries entirely.

I ran a three-day service blueprinting workshop with AMA's product, technical, and legal teams to map the actual data dependencies between steps and identify where a progressive disclosure model was feasible without creating new compliance risk.
